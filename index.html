About Me

I am a PhD student in statistics at UCLA, advised by Professor Tao Gao. Primarily, I am interested in incorporating insights about human cognition into statistical models of social intelligence. I am funded by the NSF as a Graduate Research Fellow and by UCLA's Dean Scholar Award.

I earned my bachelor's degree in statistics and cognitive psychology at Williams College, focusing on modeling attention in category learning. I also worked as a Research Assistant at Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center with Trevor Bedford. After coming to UCLA, I co-founded the Society of Women in Statistics in the fall of 2018.

When I am not doing statistics, you can find me salsa dancing, baking decorative pies, and taking photographs.

Research

Cooperation is a human hallmark of natural intelligence that has led to the development of complex cultures and social institutions. I am interesting in building computational models to explain human cooperation, augmented with information from cognitive science, game theory, and philosophy. Not only have statistical models of human minds become more powerful, but also many philosophical theories on cooperation and morality have recently been rigorously tested, providing the empirical findings for a cooperative blueprint grounded in evidence. I aim to formalize some of these findings using tools from Bayesian inference, reinforcement learning, and information theory.
